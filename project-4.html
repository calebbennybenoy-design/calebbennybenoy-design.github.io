SASI Product · South Africa

Visitor Management System

Security guards at residential complexes and office buildings across South Africa were managing visitor entry with a pen and a logbook. We designed a product to change that — without making the guard's job any harder.

My Role Product Designer
Platform Mobile Web App
Year 2025
The Product

A new SASI product for South Africa

This was not a client brief — it was a product opportunity. SASI identified that security companies across South Africa had no reliable digital solution for visitor check-in at residential complexes and commercial buildings. Guards were logging visitors by hand, making judgment calls on unregistered guests, and leaving no reliable audit trail.

I led the UX design and flow architecture on this project. A junior designer handled prototyping and usability testing.

The Problem

Paper, gut feel, and no record.

The check-in process had two distinct failure modes. For pre-registered visitors — people a resident had already approved — the guard still had no quick way to verify them beyond memory or a paper list. For unregistered visitors — a delivery driver, a one-time guest — there was no process at all.

Guards were making calls based on gut feel and writing names in a logbook that nobody ever reviewed. The process was slow, inconsistent, and left no audit trail that meant anything.

The design question was not what information should be collected. It was what does a guard need to know in 10 seconds to make a confident, correct decision.

Research

Starting with the guard, not the screen.

I started by mapping every visitor scenario a guard might face — a returning resident visitor with pre-approval, a one-time delivery, an unregistered walk-in, a rejected visitor who disputes it at the gate. Each scenario had different information needs, different emotional stakes, and a different required action.

I used Claude to stress-test each scenario and surface the edge cases — what if the visitor is in the system but with the wrong ID, what if the approval has expired, what if the guard needs to escalate. This prevented assumptions from becoming design decisions.

The flow was validated before a single screen was designed.

Design Decisions

Minimum input. Maximum confidence.

  1. 01

    Two entry paths from the start

    Separating Check-In and Register Visitor upfront mirrors how guards already think. Is this person expected or are they new? Combining both into one flow would have created confusion at the moment that matters most.

  2. 02

    ID number as primary input

    Names are ambiguous, misspelled, or go by nicknames. ID numbers are unique and unambiguous. The guard asks for an ID, types the number, gets a result. No interpretation required.

  3. 03

    Border colour as instant decision signal

    The result card communicates the answer before the guard reads a word. Green border means approved. Red means rejected. Yellow means pending. The detail below is for confirmation, not for making the decision.

  4. 04

    Rejected and pending states include Call Support

    A guard faced with an ambiguous entry decision needs a clear escalation path, not a dead end. Call Support gives them somewhere to go without having to improvise.

  5. 05

    Capture Photo disabled until form is complete

    On the manual registration flow, the photo button stays inactive until all required fields are filled. Guards cannot skip ahead to the fastest part of the process without completing the record first. Data quality enforced without a warning message.

  6. 06

    Full screen success state

    After a successful check-in, the guard does not need options or navigation. They need clear confirmation and a fast path to the next visitor. One screen. One message. One button.

Reflection

The border colour on the result card sounds obvious in hindsight. It always does. But early versions communicated status only through the pill badge — and in testing, guards were reading the text before they had processed the outcome. Moving the signal to the border meant the entire card communicated the answer the moment it appeared. That is the difference between a design that works and a design that is fast.

If I were to revisit this, I would explore a recurring visitor mode — a faster path for people who check in regularly, where the guard can confirm identity with a single tap rather than re-entering an ID every time.
