Spark Security · UAE

Incident Report Approval

When a security incident happens on the ground, the paperwork that follows is just as critical as the response. Spark Security needed a faster, clearer way for supervisors to review, assess, and sign off. From their phone, in the field, in real time.

My Role Product Designer
Platform Mobile Web App
Year 2025
The Company

About Spark Security

Spark Security is a UAE-based security company managing guards deployed across commercial sites including malls, office buildings, and public spaces. Like many companies in the sector, their incident reporting process was largely paper-based. Guards would call in incidents, fill physical forms, or send photos over WhatsApp to supervisors.

This module was part of a larger mobile operations platform built by SASI. I led the UX on this particular module alongside a junior designer who handled prototyping and usability testing.

The Problem

Too much information. Not enough clarity.

When an incident occurred on site — a medical emergency, an injury, a fire, a near miss — a guard would file a report through the Spark Security Guard app. That report then entered a supervisor's approval queue.

The challenge was that supervisors are mobile. They are managing multiple sites, often checking reports early in the morning or between shifts, on their phone, with limited time and attention. The existing process gave them too much to read and no clear path to action. Reports were delayed. Sign-offs were missed.

The core tension: the report carries a lot of information. But a supervisor scanning a queue at 6am does not need all of it immediately. The design needed to surface the right information at the right moment.

Research

Designing for the field, not the office.

The central question I started with was simple: what does a supervisor actually need to know in the seconds it takes to decide whether to act on a report? Not what information exists about the incident — but what is the minimum needed to make a confident, correct decision.

I mapped the full incident lifecycle from the guard filing the report through to the branch manager's final sign-off, identifying every decision point and every role involved.

I used Claude to stress-test edge cases — what if the supervisor is not on site, what if the incident is disputed, what if the branch manager needs to escalate. This helped define not just the UI states but the exact copy and actions needed at each outcome.

Design Decisions

Built for speed and clarity.

  1. 01

    Colour coded incident cards

    Each incident type has a colour dot — red for First Aid Medical, purple for Lost Time/Injury, blue for Observation, orange for Fire and Near Miss. Supervisors get a visual severity read before they read a single word.

  2. 02

    Accordion detail page

    Rather than one long scroll that buried the Sign action, collapsing non-critical sections keeps the page manageable. The supervisor expands only what they need.

  3. 03

    Sticky Sign CTA

    The primary action button stays fixed at the bottom of the screen regardless of which section is open. The supervisor never has to scroll to find it.

  4. 04

    GPS capture indicator

    An onsite report flag gives supervisors confidence the report was filed from the actual location, reducing the chance of backdated or off-site submissions.

  5. 05

    Tiered signature model

    Supervisor signs first. Branch Manager signature is locked until the supervisor has signed. The locked state communicates role-gating clearly without error messages or extra instructions.

  6. 06

    Collapsing search bar

    The search bar collapses to an icon by default. Most supervisors scan rather than search. Screen real estate on mobile is limited and every pixel counts.

Reflection

The sticky Sign button sounds like a small decision. It was not. Supervisors told us they felt more confident acting on a report when the action was always visible — even while they were still reading. Presence communicates readiness. That is the kind of detail that does not show up in a brief but makes all the difference in use.

If I were to revisit this, I would explore a way to flag reports that have been sitting in the queue too long — a quiet urgency indicator that prompts action without creating anxiety.
