Transguard Group · UAE

WhatsApp Operations Monitor

Transguard runs thousands of security operations across the UAE. Every deployment had its own WhatsApp group. Nobody at headquarters could see any of them.

My Role Product Designer
Platform Web Platform
Year 2025
The Company

About Transguard Group

Transguard Group is one of the UAE's largest and most trusted security and manpower companies. With a workforce of over 60,000 employees, they provide security services across some of the most high-profile locations in the country — from malls and banks to airports and VIP operations. At this scale, coordination is everything.

The Problem

Operations running blind.

Field managers naturally adopted WhatsApp groups to coordinate their teams. It was fast, familiar, and it worked on the ground. But it created a complete blind spot at the leadership level.

There was no way to know how many groups existed, what was being communicated, or whether operations were active. Every conversation was locked inside an individual manager's phone with no audit trail and no visibility for anyone above them.

The constraint: WhatsApp was deeply embedded in how guards actually worked. Any solution had to sit around the existing behaviour, not replace it.

Research

Understanding the workflow.

I started by mapping how information actually moved through the organisation. Managers were screenshotting WhatsApp threads and emailing them to HQ — a clear signal that the need for central visibility was real but unmet.

Two primary users emerged. Operations managers at HQ needed a bird's-eye view across all deployments. Compliance and admin staff needed to audit communication logs and export records for reporting.

I used Claude to model the different user scenarios and stress-test the information hierarchy — specifically what needed to surface at the list level versus what could live a level deeper on demand.

Design Decisions

Every choice had a reason.

  1. 01

    Read-only by design

    Making the system purely a monitor with no send capability. Adding a send feature would have created a parallel communication channel that confused the existing workflow. The system's job was to observe, not intervene.

  2. 02

    Table layout over cards

    Early explorations used cards. After recognising that the primary behaviour was scanning and comparing rather than browsing, I switched to a dense data table. Managers can assess 20 groups at a glance.

  3. 03

    Inline message expansion

    Clicking a message expands a detail panel inline rather than navigating away. Keeps the full log visible for context while revealing sender info, full text, and media.

  4. 04

    Split-pane media viewer

    When a message contains media, the expanded view splits — text left, media carousel right. Separating them entirely would have broken the connection between what was said and what was sent.

  5. 05

    Message type as scannable data

    Each message tagged as Text, Media, Poll, or Contact. Lets auditors filter for specific evidence without reading every message.

Reflection

The most valuable design decision on this project was not a UI choice. It was the decision to not build a send feature. Restraint is harder than addition, and here it was the right call.

If I were to revisit this, I'd explore a notification layer — alerting HQ when a group goes suddenly quiet or unusually active, rather than requiring someone to manually check. Monitoring is reactive. The next step is making it proactive.
